\title{A Correctness and Runtime Benchmark for Windowed Real-Time EEG Signal Processing:\\
Phase, Windowing, and Spectral Estimation}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Sukalyan Roy}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Avinya Neurotech\\
Email: sukalyanroyofficial@gmail.com}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Browser- and cloud-based clinical EEG review increasingly filters and re-references signals on
demand, one short window at a time. We show that this setting introduces correctness failures that
whole-signal offline practice does not anticipate, and derive deterministic criteria to prevent
them using a standard-library reference implementation evaluated against synthetic ground truth
and two open datasets (CHB-MIT, 256\,Hz; Sleep-EDF, 100\,Hz). First, filtering display windows
independently produces chunk-seam artifacts; overlap-add filtering of an extended window removes
them by about 35\,dB on synthetic data and 23--51\,dB on real data at negligible latency. Second,
zero-phase filtering (\texttt{filtfilt}) has a length floor of three filter lengths; below it, our
reference falls back to a causal path whose group delay displaces events by hundreds of
milliseconds ($\sim$500\,ms at 200\,Hz, $\sim$0.4\,s at 256\,Hz, $\sim$1\,s at 100\,Hz). We
distill the rule $(\mathrm{chunk}+2\!\cdot\!\mathrm{overlap})f_s > 3\!\cdot\!\mathrm{taps}$ and
implement a sampling-rate-aware scheduler. Third, tapering before an estimator that already tapers
biases relative band power by up to $\sim$12 percentage points for Welch and 9 for multitaper;
taper once. Finally, an 18-recording benchmark shows scheduled overlap keeps filtering zero-phase
with median per-window latency under 6\,ms, real-time factor far below one, and boundary policies
for record edges. The result is a reproducible set of deployable correctness invariants for
streaming clinical EEG DSP.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
EEG, real-time filtering, zero-phase filtering, overlap-add, group delay, quantitative EEG,

\section{Introduction}
Clinical electroencephalography (EEG) is increasingly reviewed through networked, browser- and
cloud-based viewers~\cite{weiler2023robin,currey2023epviz} that fetch and process short windows of
a recording rather than loading entire studies; clinicians page through the trace one short epoch
at a time, so only the visible window need be fetched, filtered, and re-referenced.
This shift moves standard digital-signal-processing (DSP) operations, bandpass and notch
filtering and quantitative-EEG (QEEG) feature extraction, from a whole-signal, offline setting
into a windowed, real-time setting. Operations that are textbook-correct offline can become
subtly and silently wrong under windowing: a filter that is zero-phase on a whole recording may
not be on a short window, and an estimator that is unbiased on a clean buffer may be biased once
a window is tapered upstream.

This paper characterizes that gap and derives deterministic criteria to close it. Rather than
propose a new algorithm, we take the standard streaming DSP chain as the system under test: an FIR
bandpass applied with \texttt{scipy.signal.filtfilt}, an IIR notch, and a Welch/multitaper power
spectral density (PSD) routine. We quantify where windowing breaks correctness. We use
synthetic signals with known spectra for ground truth and open clinical recordings for external
validity. We make five contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A reproducible methodology that turns a windowed EDF DSP chain into a correctness and
runtime benchmark, built only on standard libraries, with released code and data fetched at run
time.
\item A zero-phase length-floor result: below a length threshold \texttt{filtfilt} cannot run, so
a streaming filter must relax the filter or fall back to a causal path, producing a measured
event mis-timing of hundreds of milliseconds ($\sim$500\,ms at 200\,Hz, $\sim$0.4\,s at 256\,Hz,
$\sim$1\,s at 100\,Hz, rising further with FIR order); we give a deterministic guard and implement it as a
safe-window scheduler.
\item A quantification of overlap-add windowed filtering ($\sim$35\,dB seam-artifact reduction at
negligible latency), confirmed on two real datasets at different sampling rates.
\item A cascaded-tapering result for windowed PSD estimation: tapering twice biases relative band
power for both Welch and multitaper, with a one-line remedy.
\item An end-to-end EDF benchmark across 18 recordings at two sampling rates: sweeping segment
duration, FIR order, and channel count for throughput, \emph{directly measuring} per-window
latency against a display-refresh budget, and measuring boundary policies (prefetch /
reflection) that make the scheduled guarantee hold at record edges, not only interior windows.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\textbf{Streaming EEG visualization.} Networked viewers~\cite{weiler2023robin,currey2023epviz}
serve windowed segments to the client over the EDF/EDF+ format~\cite{kemp1992,kemp2003}; prior work emphasizes interactivity and scale, but
the DSP correctness of windowed filtering and feature extraction in this setting is largely
unexamined.

\textbf{Filtering and phase.} Bandpass/notch filtering is standard, and phase-distortion and
transient effects on event timing are documented for offline
analysis~\cite{widmann2015,decheveigne2019}; zero-phase (forward--backward) filtering removes phase
distortion but is non-causal and needs sufficient data length~\cite{oppenheim2009}. None
characterize the length-gated zero-phase-to-causal transition inside a streaming filter, or its
timing cost.

\textbf{Block convolution.} Block filtering is classically handled by
overlap-add/overlap-save~\cite{oppenheim2009,proakis2007}, with standard-library
routines~\cite{virtanen2020}, but is not characterized for clinical EEG streaming nor parameterized
against a zero-phase length floor.

\textbf{Spectral band power.} Relative band power via Welch~\cite{welch1967} or
multitaper~\cite{thomson1982} is a common QEEG feature with widely used
implementations~\cite{gramfort2013,virtanen2020}. Tapering is intrinsic to these estimators;
double-tapering errors and their bias on clinical relative band power are under-reported.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Reference DSP primitives}
We evaluate the conventional streaming chain implemented with standard libraries only: an FIR
bandpass (order 200, 202 taps, designed with \texttt{firwin} and a Hamming window) applied with
forward--backward zero-phase filtering (\texttt{scipy.signal.filtfilt}); an IIR notch
(\texttt{iirnotch}) applied with \texttt{filtfilt}; and a Welch/multitaper PSD routine
(\texttt{scipy.signal.welch}, \texttt{mne}). A salient and standard detail is that
\texttt{filtfilt} requires the input to exceed its default padding length,
$3\!\cdot\!\mathrm{len(taps)}$ samples; for 202 taps this pad length is 606 samples, so the
shortest valid input is 607 samples. A streaming filter that must return a shorter window must
therefore either change the filter design (fewer taps, a shorter padding length, or an IIR
alternative) or fall back to a causal filter; our reference implementation (\texttt{dsp.py}) takes
the causal \texttt{lfilter} path and makes both regimes explicit. The trade-off is that the design
changes alter the realized response, whereas the causal fallback preserves it but introduces group
delay.

\subsection{Synthetic ground truth}
We generate deterministic, seeded multichannel signals at 200\,Hz with known spectral content:
band-limited oscillations ($\delta/\theta/\alpha/\beta$) with randomized phases,
amplitude-modulated bursts, $1/f$ background, and a 50\,Hz mains component. The signal is
continuous and stationary-in-expectation, so any discontinuity in a windowed reconstruction is
attributable to the windowing strategy rather than to the signal.

\subsection{Windowing strategies and metrics}
We compare three strategies driving the \emph{same} filters: \emph{whole-signal} (zero-phase
reference), \emph{naive} per-window (each non-overlapping window filtered independently), and
\emph{overlap-add} (each window filtered within an extended window of $\pm$\,overlap seconds of
real neighbors, retaining the center). \emph{Boundary error} is the RMS error within $\pm$50\,ms
of seams versus the reference; \emph{interior error} excludes $\pm$0.6\,s. \emph{Event
mis-timing} is the shift of a 20\,ms Gaussian transient by peak location and cross-correlation.
For spectral estimation, \emph{feature equivalence} compares per-band relative power from the
estimator under test to \texttt{mne} (\texttt{psd\_array\_welch}/\texttt{psd\_array\_multitaper})
by Pearson correlation and maximum per-band discrepancy in percentage points (pp).

\subsection{Safe-window scheduler and runtime benchmark}
We implement the zero-phase guard as a scheduler (\texttt{safe\_window.py}). Given sampling rate
$f_s$, requested chunk duration $c$, requested context $o$, and FIR tap count $T$, it computes the
minimum context
\begin{equation}
o_{\min}=\frac{\max(0,\lceil(3T+1-cf_s)/2\rceil)}{f_s},
\label{eq:scheduler}
\end{equation}
and uses $\max(o,o_{\min})$ so interior windows remain in the zero-phase regime. The scheduler
also reports whether first/last record windows need an explicit boundary policy, and reports the
fallback group delay that would be exposed if the guard is ignored.

To connect correctness to deployability we run three measurements over all 18 local recordings of
both datasets, aggregated as mean\,$\pm$\,SD across the files of each dataset. (i) A
\emph{throughput} benchmark (\texttt{benchmark\_edf\_lengths.py}) loads the first $N$ seconds of an
EDF, runs filtering and Welch relative-band-power extraction, and reports load/filter/feature
time, array memory, and the real-time factor (processing time over segment duration), sweeping
segment duration (30--600\,s), FIR order (100--400), and channel count (1--16) for the four paths
(naive, fixed 0.5\,s overlap-add, scheduled-overlap, whole-signal). Timing runs without memory
instrumentation, which would otherwise inflate wall time. (ii) A \emph{latency} benchmark
(\texttt{benchmark\_latency.py}) instead filters one display window at a time and times each
all-channel request independently, reporting the median/p95/max distribution against a 60\,Hz
display-refresh budget, a direct measurement of per-window cost rather than an amortized one.
(iii) A \emph{boundary-policy} experiment (\texttt{exp\_edge\_policy.py}) measures the edge-window
error of the causal fallback, a reflection zero-phase policy, and real-context prefetching
against the whole-signal zero-phase reference.

\subsection{Real data and reproducibility}
We confirm the filtering results on two open datasets with different populations, montages, and
sampling rates: PhysioNet CHB-MIT~\cite{shoeb2009,goldberger2000} (pediatric epilepsy, bipolar,
256\,Hz; first recording of each of 10 subjects) and Sleep-EDF Expanded~\cite{kemp2000sleepedf,
goldberger2000} (sleep, Fpz-Cz/Pz-Oz, 100\,Hz; \textbf{8} subjects). The two sampling rates
make the floor's $f_s$-dependence explicit. Experiments ran in a fixed environment (Python 3.12;
NumPy 2.4.1; SciPy 1.17.0; MNE 1.11.0). The reference
implementation, synthetic generators, metrics, and figure scripts are released; raw EDFs are
fetched from PhysioNet at run time and are not redistributed.

\section{Results}
\subsection{R1: Overlap-add eliminates window-seam artifacts}
With 8\,s windows, naive filtering concentrates error at seams (boundary RMSE 14.93\,$\mu$V) while
leaving the interior near-exact; overlap-add with 0.5\,s context reduces boundary RMSE to
0.275\,$\mu$V (34.7\,dB), and 1.0\,s renders it negligible (Fig.~\ref{fig:seam}). This holds across
display-window sizes, and the per-window overhead is within a few percent of naive filtering, so
the correction is effectively free.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_seam.png}
\caption{Filtered synthetic EEG across a 1\,s window seam. Naive per-window filtering departs from
the whole-signal ground truth at the seam; overlap-add (0.5\,s context) tracks it.}
\label{fig:seam}
\end{figure}

\subsection{R2: A zero-phase length floor and a design criterion}
The bandpass is zero-phase via \texttt{filtfilt} only when the window exceeds
$3\!\cdot\!\mathrm{taps} = 606$ samples; for this 202-tap filter the shortest valid input is
607 samples (3.04\,s at 200\,Hz). A shorter window cannot be filtered zero-phase. A streaming
filter processes a span of $\mathrm{chunk}+2\!\cdot\!\mathrm{overlap}$:
large windows clear the floor and are zero-phase, but a small window (e.g.\ a 1\,s chunk with
0.5\,s overlap spans $400 < 607$ samples) falls below it. On the causal fallback the error is
large \emph{throughout} the window (interior RMSE 30.7\,$\mu$V), not only at seams. The
deterministic guard is to keep
\begin{equation}
(\mathrm{chunk}+2\!\cdot\!\mathrm{overlap})\,f_s \;>\; 3\!\cdot\!\mathrm{len(taps)},
\label{eq:criterion}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ at least 1.02\,s of overlap each side for a 1\,s chunk at 200\,Hz.

\subsection{R3: Magnitude fidelity and event mis-timing}
On the zero-phase path the magnitude response realizes the design: interior passband (1--25\,Hz)
gain $-0.64\pm1.54$\,dB, with the 50\,Hz notch and 60\,Hz stopband below the $-120$\,dB
measurement floor. The timing is regime-dependent: a 20\,ms transient is
unshifted (0\,ms) on the zero-phase path but is displaced by $+500$\,ms on the causal fallback,
equal to the uncompensated linear-phase FIR group delay
$(\mathrm{taps}-1)/2/f_s\approx502$\,ms (Fig.~\ref{fig:shift}). Combined with R2, whenever a
window is short enough to force the fallback, displayed events, central to spike review and
annotation alignment, are mis-timed by about half a second.

\subsection{R4: Generalization across FIR order and robustness}
The pad length equals $3T$ for tap count $T$, the shortest valid zero-phase input is $3T+1$, and
the fallback shift equals $(T-1)/2/f_s$; all scale with FIR order (Table~\ref{tab:order}).
Sweeping the total filtered length through the floor shows boundary error collapse sharply at the
threshold (Fig.~\ref{fig:transition}). Across 24 seeds the overlap-add reduction is
$34.3\pm2.4$\,dB (naive $14.4\pm3.9\,\mu$V; overlap-add $0.271\pm0.032\,\mu$V), confirming
robustness and generalizing criterion~\eqref{eq:criterion} to any FIR order.

\subsection{R5: A cascaded-tapering pitfall in spectral estimation}
Welch and multitaper estimators taper internally. Applying a Hann window to the analysis window
\emph{before} such an estimator tapers the signal twice and biases relative band power. A
doubly-tapered Welch estimate differs from MNE \texttt{psd\_array\_welch} by up to 11.9\,pp on the
dominant band; tapering once (letting Welch apply its per-segment Hann) matches MNE to 0.64\,pp
($r=0.99997$, Table~\ref{tab:bp}). The bias is estimator-agnostic: pre-windowing before a
multitaper estimator biases relative band power by up to 9.1\,pp, and removing it recovers the
plain multitaper reference. The remedy is to taper exactly once.

\subsection{R6: Multi-subject, two-dataset real-data confirmation}
On the first recording of \textbf{10} CHB-MIT subjects (256\,Hz), overlap-add reduces seam
boundary RMSE by \textbf{$50.6\pm2.2$}\,dB, comparable to or larger than synthetic, with near-zero
interior error confirming zero-phase behavior (Fig.~\ref{fig:real}). The pad length is 606
samples, so the shortest valid zero-phase span is 607 samples, i.e.\ 2.37\,s at 256\,Hz; because
it is fixed in samples its duration scales with $f_s$. The
fallback shift, measured by cross-correlation against the zero-phase output, is
\textbf{$389\pm7$}\,ms, matching the predicted $(\mathrm{taps}-1)/2/f_s$
(|measured$-$theory| $=$ \textbf{$4.0\pm6.5$}\,ms). Per-subject values are consistent
(Table~\ref{tab:ms}): the overlap-add reduction is 47--55\,dB across subjects and the fallback
shift clusters tightly around the predicted group delay.

The results are not specific to one dataset or sampling rate. On \textbf{8} Sleep-EDF
subjects (100\,Hz, a different population and montage), overlap-add reduces seam boundary RMSE by
\textbf{$23.4\pm2.0$}\,dB and the fallback shift is \textbf{$1001\pm2$}\,ms, again matching the
predicted group delay (|measured$-$theory| $=$ \textbf{$3.8\pm2.2$}\,ms). The $f_s$-dependence is
explicit: the same 606-sample pad length is \textbf{6.06}\,s at 100\,Hz, so the shortest valid
span is 6.07\,s (vs.\ 2.37\,s at 256\,Hz), and the fallback mis-times events by $\sim$1\,s rather
than $\sim$0.4\,s: the lower the sampling rate, the longer both the floor and the mis-timing.

\subsection{R7: EDF runtime benchmark and the scheduled-overlap guard}
The scheduler turns the criterion into a deployable configuration rather than a post-hoc warning.
We benchmark all 18 local recordings, 10 CHB-MIT (256\,Hz, 8 channels) and 8 Sleep-EDF (100\,Hz,
2 channels), over segment durations of 30--600\,s, reporting mean\,$\pm$\,SD across the files of
each dataset (\texttt{edf\_length\_benchmark.csv}).

\emph{The scheduler is sampling-rate aware.} With 1\,s chunks and requested 0.5\,s context, the
configured interior span is below the 607-sample floor at both rates, so the scheduler raises
context automatically: to 0.6875\,s per side at 256\,Hz (a 608-sample span), but to 2.54\,s per
side at 100\,Hz, because the fixed 607-sample floor is a far larger fraction of a lower-rate
stream. This is the runtime face of the $f_s$-dependence in R6. The guarantee is for
\emph{interior} windows, which have real context on both sides; the first and last window of a
record have one-sided context only (a 432-sample span at 256\,Hz, 354 at 100\,Hz, both below the
floor), so record edges still require prefetching extra context or an explicit boundary policy.
The scheduler reports this edge status rather than silently mixing regimes.

\emph{Throughput.} Processing time scales linearly with duration (Fig.~\ref{fig:edfbench},
Table~\ref{tab:edf}). On the longest (600\,s) CHB-MIT segments, scheduled overlap takes
$3.05\pm0.06$\,s end-to-end (real-time factor $5.1\times10^{-3}$, i.e.\ 18\,s per EEG-hour); the
unsafe fixed-overlap baseline is about $2.4\times$ faster ($1.25\pm0.02$\,s) but stays in the
causal-fallback regime, and the offline whole-signal reference is fastest ($0.79\pm0.01$\,s).
Every streaming path runs two-to-three orders of magnitude faster than real time; the guardrail's
extra cost over the unsafe path is real but small in absolute terms. Peak array working memory
stays modest ($\approx$20\,MB for a 600\,s 8-channel segment). Per-window interactivity is treated
separately in R8.

\emph{Order and channel scaling.} Because the scheduler grows context with the floor, the cost of
correctness rises with FIR order: at 256\,Hz a 120\,s segment costs 0.37\,s at order 100 (0.5\,s
context) but 1.89\,s at order 400 (1.86\,s context), while the whole-signal reference is flat at
$\sim$0.17\,s (Fig.~\ref{fig:edfscale}, left); at 100\,Hz the order-400 scheduler needs 5.54\,s of
context per side. Processing time is linear in channel count for every method
(Fig.~\ref{fig:edfscale}, right). The benchmark is single-machine and should be repeated on target
hardware before product latency claims, but the ordering of the methods and the linear scaling are
machine-independent.

\subsection{R8: Measured per-window latency}
The benchmark above reports whole-segment throughput. Interactivity, however, is governed by the
cost of a single window, so we measure it directly (\texttt{benchmark\_latency.py}): we filter the
signal \emph{one display window at a time}, timing each window's all-channel request
independently, and report the distribution rather than an amortized mean.
On CHB-MIT (256\,Hz, 8 channels) the scheduled path costs a median 4.2\,ms to filter a 1\,s window
and 5.7\,ms including per-window Welch band power, with a worst-case 95th percentile of 6.4\,ms; on
Sleep-EDF (100\,Hz, 2 channels) the figures are 1.1 and 1.5\,ms. Every method, on both datasets,
stays within a single 60\,Hz display refresh (16.7\,ms), so the scheduled guardrail is
interactively imperceptible even at its p95. This directly measured latency agrees with the
amortized estimate from R7, confirming the throughput figure was not hiding a per-window cost.

\subsection{R9: Making the record edges operational}
R7's guarantee is for interior windows; the first/last window of a record has one-sided context
and would otherwise fall to the causal regime. We measure three boundary policies for the edge
window against the whole-signal zero-phase reference (\texttt{exp\_edge\_policy.py},
Fig.~\ref{fig:edge}). For a window in the interior of a recording (a view whose left edge is not
the record start), \emph{prefetching} real neighbouring samples to clear the floor makes the edge
window essentially exact: boundary RMSE $0.001\,\mu$V versus $56\,\mu$V for the unguarded causal
fallback on CHB-MIT (a $94$\,dB reduction), and likewise on Sleep-EDF. At the \emph{true} record
boundary, where no neighbouring samples exist, a reflection zero-phase policy avoids the causal
fallback and reduces the edge error by $7$--$16$\,dB, but a residual boundary error remains
(CHB-MIT $17.9\pm58.4\,\mu$V; Sleep-EDF $4.0\pm4.5\,\mu$V) because reflection only approximates the
absent samples. The recommendation is therefore deterministic: prefetch real context when it
exists (which makes interior-of-record edges exact), reflect-pad at true record boundaries to
avoid the causal regime, and never silently take the causal path. The scheduled configuration is
then operationally zero-phase at interior windows, with reduced (not eliminated) error at the two
true record boundaries.

\section{Discussion}
The results reduce to a small set of correctness invariants for streaming clinical EEG DSP.
(1)~\emph{Windowing:} always supply real context and retain only the center (overlap-add).
(2)~\emph{Zero-phase length:} enforce criterion~\eqref{eq:criterion} so the filter never leaves
the zero-phase regime, and make any fallback observable; otherwise event timing can be off by
hundreds of milliseconds. The safe-window scheduler implements this invariant by construction, the
throughput and directly-measured latency benchmarks (R7, R8) show its cost is operationally small
(per-window latency under one display refresh), and a boundary policy (R9) extends the guarantee to
record edges. (3)~\emph{Spectral estimation:} taper exactly once. These are
implementation invariants, not new algorithms, yet each is violated by a plausible windowed
configuration, and each violation is silent in the sense that the output looks like a valid
signal or spectrum.

\emph{Threats to validity.} R1--R4 rest on deterministic properties of the implementation (the
\texttt{filtfilt} length floor; linear-phase FIR group delay) and hold independent of signal
content; we nonetheless confirm them on multiple real recordings (R6). R5 follows from the
algebra of cascaded tapering and is reproduced for two estimator families. R7--R9 are
single-machine runtime measurements; the method ordering, linear scaling, and boundary-policy
error reductions are machine-independent, but the absolute times should be repeated on the target
deployment hardware before making product latency claims. We evaluate one reference
implementation, but the invariants follow from properties of \texttt{filtfilt},
linear-phase FIR group delay, and Welch/multitaper tapering common to widely used libraries, and
are relevant to medical-device software life-cycle assurance~\cite{iec62304}.

\section{Conclusion}
Moving routine EEG DSP into a windowed, real-time setting introduces silent correctness failures
that offline practice does not anticipate. Evaluating a standard-library reference chain against
synthetic ground truth and multiple open recordings, we quantify three (seam artifacts, a
zero-phase length-floor fallback whose event mis-timing is $\sim$500\,ms at 200\,Hz and
$\sim$0.4--1\,s on real data at 256 and 100\,Hz, and a cascaded-tapering spectral bias) and give deterministic,
low-cost remedies, implemented as a safe-window scheduler and benchmarked across 18 recordings.
Future work extends to larger cohorts, deployment hardware, and montage-level correctness.
